Interpretation:
{% if code == "TYPHIDOT" %}
  • Typhidot detects IgM and IgG antibodies against the 50 kDa outer-membrane protein of Salmonella typhi.
  • IgM rises within the first week of fever and disappears by 6 weeks; IgG rises later and persists for years.
  • Compared to the Widal test it is more specific in early infection and less affected by vaccination.
  • Confirm acute infection with blood / bone-marrow culture, especially before starting empirical therapy.
  • Typhidot can remain positive in past infection; correlate with clinical features.
{% elif code == "DEN-IGGM" %}
  • IgM antibodies appear 4–5 days after fever onset and persist 2–3 months — marker of recent infection.
  • IgG appears within 7–10 days in primary infection and rises rapidly in secondary infection due to immunological memory.
  • An IgM-positive, IgG-positive pattern indicates a secondary infection, which carries the highest risk of dengue hemorrhagic fever and dengue shock syndrome.
  • Combine with NS1 antigen, platelet count and hematocrit for staging and warning-sign assessment.
  • Negative serology in early illness does not exclude dengue — repeat in 5–7 days.
{% elif code == "MP-AG" %}
  • The rapid antigen test detects HRP-2 (specific to P. falciparum) and pLDH / aldolase (pan-malaria, for P. vivax).
  • Sensitivity drops at parasite density < 100/µL — confirm with peripheral smear and quantification.
  • HRP-2 may persist for 2–4 weeks after successful treatment, so a positive Pf result in a treated patient does not always indicate active infection.
  • P. falciparum infection mandates urgent assessment for severe malaria (cerebral involvement, ARDS, AKI, hypoglycaemia, severe anaemia).
{% elif code == "MP-SMEAR" %}
  • Microscopy of Giemsa- or Leishman-stained thick and thin smears remains the gold standard for malaria diagnosis.
  • Thick smear — concentrated, used for detection (10–30× more sensitive than thin smear).
  • Thin smear — used for species identification and parasite density (% parasitised RBCs).
  • Trophozoites, schizonts and gametocytes may be seen depending on stage; gametocyte-only smears indicate carrier state, not active illness.
  • A single negative smear does not exclude malaria — repeat every 8–12 hours for 48 hours when clinical suspicion remains.
{% else %}
  • Correlate clinically. Reference ranges are for adult population unless otherwise stated.
{% endif %}
